
### S5 modal logic as epistemic logic

Discussion of [[S5 modal logic]] as [[epistemic logic]]:

* [[Joseph Y. Halpern]], [[Yoram Moses]], §2.3 in: *A guide to completeness and complexity for modal logics of knowledge and belief*, Artificial Intelligence **54** 3 (1992) 319-379 $[$<a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(92)90049-4">doi:10.1016/0004-3702(92)90049-4</a>$]$

> p. 35: "in a precise sense the S5 properties completely characterize our definition of knowledge"

* [[Joseph Y. Halpern]], *Should knowledge entail belief?*, Journal of Philosophical Logic **25** 5 (1996) 483-494  $[$[jstor:30226583](https://www.jstor.org/stable/30226583), [philpapers:HALSKE](https://philpapers.org/rec/HALSKE)$]$

* Dov Samet, *S5 knowledge without partitions*, Synthese **172** (2010) 145–155 $[$[doi:10.1007/s11229-009-9469-0](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11229-009-9469-0)$]$

  > p. 1: "Propositional epistemic logic S5 is a well-known modal logic which is suitable for representing and reasoning about the knowledge of a single agent"

* Rasmus Rendsvig, John Symons, *Epistemic Logic*, The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (2011) $[$[web](https://plato.stanford.edu/cgi-bin/encyclopedia/archinfo.cgi?entry=logic-epistemic)$]$

> "[Fagin, Halpern, Moses, and Vardi (1995)](FaginHalpernMosesVardi95) and many others use S5 for knowledge"

* Rachel Boddy, *Epistemic Issues and Group Knowledge*, Amsterdam (2014) $[$[pdf](https://eprints.illc.uva.nl/id/eprint/921/1/MoL-2014-03.text.pdf)$]$

> p. 13: "Formal approaches to epistemology – such as game
theory and computer science – typically assume the S5 conditions for knowledge, which is (partly) explained by the convenient formal properties of the logic. Philosophers typically opt for a weaker notion. Hintikka (1962), for instance, argues that the proper logic for knowledge is the modal system S4"
